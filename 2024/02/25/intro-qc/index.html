1. Concept of quantum computer

1-1 Motivation

In the Schrödinger picture, the evolution of a closed quantum system is described by
$$
|\psi’(t)\rangle = U(t)|\psi(0)\rangle
$$
where $U(t)\equiv U$ is some unitary operator, i.e. $UU^{\dagger}=I$.

There is a fact that exactly simulating an arbitrary quantum system on classical computers need exponentially large resources! How to bypass/overcome this problem?

A genius idea is to make $U$ controllable. If we have a controllable quantum system, i.e. we can evolve $|\psi\rangle$ into any $|\psi’\rangle$ as we want, then we are able to investigate the target quantum system by mimicing and calculating its behaviors and properties.

Now the problem becomes

  1. How to physically build a controllable quantum system (hardware level)?

  2. With what a specific $U$ can we evolve $|\psi\rangle$ to $|\psi’\rangle$ (algorithm or software level)?

Such a controllable quantum system is called a quantum computer.

Algorithms for finding/designing $U$ for some problems on a quantum computer are called quantum algorithms.

1-3 Why do we name it after a “computer”?

Notice that the computation can be interpreted by
$$
\text{input: }x_0 \xrightarrow{f_0(x)} x_1
\xrightarrow{f_2(x)} \cdots \xrightarrow{f_{m -1 }(x)} \text{output: }x_{m}
$$

where

  • ${x_j}$ are some classical data
  • ${f_j(x)}$ are some classical operations, which form a classical algorithm
  • The process is realized on a physical platform (computer)

On the other hand, a controllable quantum system performs
$$
|\psi_m\rangle = U_{m-1}\cdots U_{1} U_0 |\psi_0\rangle
$$

  • ${\psi_j}$ are can analogously called quantum datum
  • ${U_j}$ are quantum operations, which form quantum algorithm
  • The process is realized on a physical platform (quantum computer)

1-4 From classical computing to quantum computing

Since the theory of classical computing is mature, we would like to analogously develop the theory of quantum computing base on it.

In classical computing, the elemental computational unit is called a bit, which is a binary number
$$
c \in {0, 1}
$$
A bit can be physically realized by a classical two-level system like two different voltage levels or the presence or absence of a signal on a semiconductor chip.

The first step to generalize the classical theory to quantum is to introduce a quantum counterpart, which we call a quantum bit, or qubit.

Since quantum mechanics permits superposition, therefore we can write it in the form
$$
q=\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle
$$
where $\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb{C}$ and $|\alpha|^2+|\beta|^2=1$.

Physically, a qubit can by realized by some quantum two-level system like the Rydberg atom.

2. Single qubit

2-1 Qubit

Now we make the definition of a qubit more specific.

Any qubit $|q\rangle$ can be written as
$$
|q\rangle = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}|0\rangle + \sin\frac{\theta}{2}e^{i\phi} |1\rangle
$$
where
$$
|0\rangle =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \ 0
\end{bmatrix} \
|1\rangle =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \ 1
\end{bmatrix}
$$
therefore
$$
|q\rangle = \begin{bmatrix}
\cos(\theta/2) \
\sin(\theta/2) e^{i\phi}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

$|q\rangle$ has a 1-1 mapping with points on a Bloch sphere, represented by a unit vector:
$$
\vec{r}=(\sin\theta\cos\phi,\sin\theta\sin\phi,\cos\theta)
$$
